Technology And Institutions Teaching Cohort Design

A small pilot design for teaching institutional reasoning around AI and technology policy.

The teaching cohort is a small pilot for the open technology-and-institutions module. It is not a certificate program, fellowship, or institutional training product. Its purpose is narrower: test whether students and early-career readers can use the project's vocabulary to reason through concrete technology-governance decisions.

The cohort builds from three existing assets: the EPPAI course, the AI Governance Primers, and the Governance Stress-Test method. EPPAI established the classroom arc. The primers made the concepts reusable. The cohort tests whether those concepts can become a public teaching module.

Audience

The pilot cohort should be small: 12-18 participants.

Target participants:

  • public-policy students and recent graduates;
  • technologists interested in governance and public institutions;
  • aspiring civil servants and early-career public-policy professionals;
  • researchers or analysts working on AI, DPI, development, or technology policy.

Recruitment can run through Yukti's early readership, EPPAI alumni and Kautilya-adjacent networks, and direct professional networks. No institutional partnership is required to fill 12-18 seats.

The cohort should not depend on access to serving IAS officers or formal public-agency partnerships. If public officials or agency staff participate later, that is useful, but not load-bearing for the grant.

Format

Recommended format: four sessions over four to six weeks, with one short written exercise before the first session and one applied memo after the final session.

Each session can run 90 minutes:

SessionRoleFocusExercise
1InterpreterHow AI is framed in public discourse; why access, adoption, and capability are different claimsRewrite a public AI claim into mechanism language
2EconomistHow AI changes cost structures, verification bottlenecks, delegation, market concentration, and institutional constraintsMap mechanism -> policy instrument -> institutional constraint
3Policy DesignerHow governance stress-tests turn AI risks into institutional questionsWork through the Samagra Vedika stress-test template
4StrategistHow India and other middle powers navigate compute, DPI, sovereignty, efficiency, and dependencyDraft a 1-2 page governance memo on a selected case

The four-role structure comes from EPPAI: interpreter, economist, policy designer, strategist. It gives the cohort a progression from diagnosis to institutional design, rather than a survey of AI policy topics.

Public Materials

The grant-period deliverable is a public teaching module, not a private class.

Public outputs should include:

  • syllabus and session plan;
  • reading sequence drawn from Yukti essays, AI Governance Primers, and selected external materials;
  • teaching slides or notes where shareable;
  • governance stress-test worksheet;
  • case prompts for Samagra Vedika, public-sector AI deployment thresholds, DPI-linked recourse, and compute / sovereignty choices;
  • short post-cohort note on what changed in the module after feedback.

Participant submissions should remain private unless explicit permission is given. The public artifact is the module and the learning from the pilot, not student work.

Feedback And Evaluation

The cohort should use light pedagogical feedback, not a formal research design.

Before the first session, participants answer one short diagnostic prompt: identify the governance problem in a technology decision and name the missing institutional capability.

After the final session, participants answer a parallel prompt or submit a short governance memo. The comparison is used to improve the module: whether participants move from descriptive claims to mechanism precision, from generic tools to instrument design, and from policy preference to institutional feasibility.

This is not a human-subjects experiment and should not be described as empirical evidence about deliberation or learning outcomes. It is a teaching pilot and feedback loop for public materials.

Success Criteria

By the end of the pilot, the cohort should produce four things:

  1. a revised public syllabus;
  2. a reusable stress-test worksheet;
  3. at least two improved case prompts;
  4. a short note on what the pilot revealed about teaching institutional reasoning for AI and technology policy.

The larger test is whether a serious learner can move from "AI policy should require audits" to a sharper institutional question: who can evaluate the system, what evidence would stop deployment, who owns recourse, and what the institution learns after failure.
